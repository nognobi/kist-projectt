How to be safe from scams

Taxi & Transport Scams:


Many taxi drivers in Nepal refuse to use meters and instead charge high fixed fares, especially in tourist areas like Thamel, the airport, and bus stations. This leads to overcharging, making transportation more expensive than it should be. Similarly, rickshaw drivers in places like Thamel and Patan often demand much higher prices from tourists than from locals. When traveling by tourist buses to destinations like Pokhara or Chitwan, unnecessary "fees" may be added to the ticket price. To avoid these scams, always ask taxi drivers to use the meter, negotiate the fare before the ride, or use ride-hailing apps like Pathao and Tootle for fair pricing.

Fake Trekking Permits & Guides:


Nepal is famous for its trekking routes, but tourists often fall victim to fake trekking permits and overpriced guides. Unauthorized agents may sell permits that are not valid, or trekking guides may overcharge and offer poor services. Some unregistered guides might even abandon tourists midway through their trek. To prevent this, always get your permits from official sources such as the Nepal Tourism Board and book trekking tours through reputed agencies with verified reviews.

Overpriced Souvenirs & Handicrafts:


Shops in tourist areas like Thamel often inflate the prices of souvenirs such as pashminas, singing bowls, and handicrafts. Many tourists end up paying double or triple the actual price because they are unaware of the local rates. Bargaining is expected in Nepal, so to get a fair deal, tourists should offer a lower price and negotiate. Visiting local markets like Asan or New Road can also help find better prices.

Fake Charity & Donation Scams:


Some individuals dressed as monks or charity workers may approach tourists and ask for donations, claiming they are for an orphanage or a good cause. In reality, many of these scams do not support real charities, and the money is taken for personal gain. To avoid falling for such scams, tourists should only donate to registered NGOs and well-known organizations instead of giving money to people on the streets.

Currency Exchange & ATM Fraud:


Tourists exchanging money at unauthorized counters may receive fake or outdated notes. Additionally, some ATMs in tourist areas charge high withdrawal fees, while others may even skim card details to commit fraud. To stay safe, it is best to exchange money only at official banks or authorized exchange counters. Using ATMs inside banks or shopping malls is also safer than using isolated street ATMs.

"Free" Gifts or Blessings Scam:


In some areas, scammers may offer tourists a "free" gift, such as a bracelet, tika, or flower, only to later demand money aggressively. Many tourists accept these small gifts out of politeness, not realizing that they will be pressured into paying. The best way to avoid this scam is to never accept unsolicited gifts from strangers and to walk away politely if someone insists on giving something for free.

Fake Tour Packages & Adventure Activities:


Some travel agencies overcharge tourists for adventure activities like mountain flights, paragliding, and safaris. In worse cases, scammers take money for a tour or activity and then disappear without providing the service. To prevent this, tourists should always book through reputed travel agencies with good online reviews and check official prices on company websites before making a payment.

Entry Fee & Fake Guide Scams:


Many cultural sites in Nepal, such as Swayambhunath and Bhaktapur Durbar Square, have separate entrance fees for foreigners, which are significantly higher than those for locals. While these official fees are unavoidable, some unofficial guides approach tourists and demand extra fees for services that are not necessary. To avoid this, visitors should check official entry fees beforehand and decline any unnecessary services from unofficial guides.

Overpriced Shopping & Adventure Equipment:


Tourists looking to buy clothes, spices, electronics, or trekking gear often face higher prices in local markets, especially if they do not bargain. Vendors in places like Durbar Square, Asan Bazaar, and Lakeside Pokhara tend to overcharge tourists who are unfamiliar with local prices. When renting or buying trekking gear, prices may also be inflated near tourist hubs or starting points of trekking routes. To avoid overpaying, it is important to research prices in advance, compare costs at multiple stores, and negotiate respectfully when shopping.
